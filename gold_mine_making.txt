Opening a new gold mine is a long-term endeavor that typically takes 10 to 20 years from the initial discovery of a deposit to the first production of gold. [1, 2, 3] 
Recent data indicates that this timeline is stretching; the average lead time for mines starting between 2020 and 2023 was approximately 17.9 years, compared to 12.7 years in the mid-2000s. [4, 5] 
Development Timeline by Stage
The process is divided into several complex phases, each requiring significant capital and regulatory approval: [6, 7, 8] 

* Exploration & Discovery (1–10 years): Geologists identify potential deposits through surveys and drilling. Less than 0.1% of prospected sites actually lead to a productive mine.
* Feasibility & Permitting (1–5+ years): Once a deposit is confirmed, companies must prove it is economically viable and obtain environmental and local licenses. In the U.S., the permitting process alone can take close to 10 years.
* Construction (1–5 years): Building the physical infrastructure, including roads, processing plants, and the mine itself (shafts for underground or pits for surface mining).
* Production (10–30 years): The active operational phase where ore is extracted and refined. [2, 3, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15] 

Key Factors Affecting the Timeline

* Jurisdiction: Pro-mining regions with established regulatory frameworks generally move faster than remote or "anti-mining" areas.
* Mine Type: Open-pit mines often have longer exploration and permitting phases compared to underground mines.
* Infrastructure: Remote locations require the construction of entire power grids, water supplies, and worker housing before mining can begin. [9, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20]
